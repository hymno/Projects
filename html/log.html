7/3/2017 11:07 PST

Reading Magaña-Loaiza et al, Exotic looped trajectories of photons in three-slit interference. Super cool. The looped trajectories come about from solving the Helmholtz equation for the light waves \( \psi \) $$(\nabla + k)\psi = 0$$ using the following propagator in a Feynman path integral approach: $$K(\vec{r}_1, \vec{r}_2) = \int \mathcal{D}[x(\vec{s})] \exp \bigg( ik \int d\vec{s} \bigg)$$ The above \( K \) perturbatively expands over the allowable paths \( x \): $$K = K_1 + K_2 + ...$$ \( K_1 \) accounts for the normal paths for Borne's rule, while \( K_2 + K_3 + ... \) contain things like the looped trajectories! In the paper, they do fancy things to the geometry and conditions of the triple slits in order to amplify near field effects which increases the probability densities of the looped trajectories.

Flying back to Oakland airport. Want to read more about Clifford algebras but there's no wifi on the plane, so I spent a bit of time working on the website. There's a home page now ... whoop dee doo.

On a more interesting note, I thought of a nice set of gifts to give my graduate letter writers. Kuhn's book mentions some of the classic texts written by the great scientists of the renaissance era such as Principia and Physica, but what took a hold of me were the more obscure texts that were actually pivotal to certain momentous paradigm shifts. Of these, there were Priestly's Experiments and Observations on Different Kinds of Air, Benjamin Franklin's Electricity, and especially Lavoisier's writings like Reflexions sur le phlogistique. These texts fought against ideas that are now considered archaic relics of the old sciences, and I think my physicist recommenders would have an appreciation for the history contained in these writings.

Lavoisier was particularly awesome. He fought tooth and nail to eliminate phlogiston theory (though to it's credit, it's a fascinating theory to read about, along with some other wacky theories like the Four Humours medieval theory of medicine; references to these now-obscure alchemies is another valuable aspect of these writings IMO) and as I understand he was also politically outspoken during the French revolution. Then some people cut off his head with a guillotine, which is no fun and his death was protested by his contemporaries, some of the greatest scientists in history.

For example, when I began coursework at UCSC as an undergrad I had also been reading Erik Verlinde's paper on entropic or emergent gravity. I had been inspired also by the emergence of the London dispersion force from the theory of electron clouds, and so I devised a metaphysical model in which gravity arose from interlocking interference patterns of the summed electrostatic potentials of atoms and molecules, forming a lattice self-reaction akin to chinese finger traps between bodies. This was supposed to be a kind of hidden electric field that would be globally neutral but on the order of the Planck length showed local variation. Of course it's completely stupid because there are particles that don't interact with EM at all which still exhibit gravity, plus I bet if I tried to for a single mathematical expression of this I would soon find immediate problems with such a stupid, stupid idea.

To say that curved space is matter and energy.

Well, I'm not going to get into this idea too much but I've got a concrete expression of this metaphysic that I'm going to explore. The actual benefit of doing this is self-education, since I'm sure the chance that it will produce any solution or tractable paradigm is 0%. But it's still fun.


6/24/2017 9:30 AM (HST)

This is the first journal entry. I'm writing this as I'm sitting on a plane heading for Maui. I'm reading Kuhn's book on scientific revolutions and it's getting me to think about the psychologist Dr. Jordan Peterson's notions of truth; he says there's a scientific truth and a moral truth. I was very resistant to it, but the statement became more digestible as I remembered that truth is never absolute, so why not have different varieties? As humans, we decide truth. Someone makes a statement about anything (scientific, moral, or otherwise) and the rest listen and see whether or not the evidence produces logical contradictions. And if not, we just decide that that's the truth. There's no answer key or crib sheet for the truth hiding out in the crab nebula somewhere, it's just the collection of all statements that people have ever decided are true. And they're never absolutely true, but perhaps some are what could be called "true enough", or true in the pragmatic sense that they wouldn't exhibit any meaningful contradictions or inspire decisions that lead us to harm.

That being said, moral truth seems much more difficult to get to the bottom of than scientific truth. What I think Peterson means by moral truth is whether something is right or wrong, and varying degrees in between. But to know that, we have to know very intimately what people want. To assess if something is good one has to know for whom it may be good, and then establish the set of axioms that define a preexisting set of things that are good for that person. Then if you have N people you have to somehow figure out a way to "average out" those axioms through an optimization process that minimizes the loss of goodness over N. And if you can't do that very well the next thing to try is to segment the population into different groups and cultures of people with locally optimized moralities and well, this is what's already naturally taken course over thousands of years of human history.

What I'm trying to get at is that our definition of truth should be sympathetic to the impossibility of absolute truth. More than that, I would straightaway define truth as the collection of thoughts most optimized to agree with physical outcomes. But that's the thing; criteria aside, the physical manifestation of truth is a thought.
